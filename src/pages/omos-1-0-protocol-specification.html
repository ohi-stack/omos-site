Status of This Memo

This document defines OMOS-1.0 as a draft protocol framework for semantic alignment, interpretive consistency, and behavioral interoperability across heterogeneous intelligences, including human users, software agents, and robotic systems.

This document is not an Internet Engineering Task Force standard. It is a proprietary-origin protocol specification published for implementation, testing, licensing, controlled ecosystem adoption, and technical review.

Gregorian Time remains the controlling civil and institutional date. OneGodian Time is included as a supplemental internal sequencing layer.

Abstract
Protocol Summary

The OneGodian Metaphysical Operating System Protocol, abbreviated OMOS-1.0, defines a shared alignment layer for communication and interaction among entities that may differ in ontology, belief structure, embodiment, optimization logic, or decision architecture.

OMOS-1.0 provides:

  • a semantic normalization model;
  • a six-stage decision protocol;
  • a dignity-preserving behavioral standard;
  • a conflict-reduction method;
  • a machine-readable exchange structure for units of meaning.

OMOS-1.0 functions analogously to a protocol layer. It does not require identical beliefs across participants. It establishes rules for translating, aligning, and responding across differing systems.

§ 1
Conventions and Terminology

The key words MUST, MUST NOT, REQUIRED, SHALL, SHALL NOT, SHOULD, SHOULD NOT, MAY, and OPTIONAL in this document are to be interpreted as normative requirements.

1.1 Defined Terms

Agent — Any software, human, or embodied system participating in OMOS exchange.

Entity — Any addressable participant within the protocol domain.

Meaning Unit — A structured packet of semantic content submitted for analysis, interpretation, or response.

Alignment State — The computed relationship between a received input and the operative principles of OMOS.

Dignity Constraint — A mandatory behavioral rule prohibiting degrading, dehumanizing, or needlessly destructive interpretation or output.

Unified Origin Principle — A foundational abstraction stating that all participating entities are processed as members of a shared field of value attribution.

Common Ground Extraction — The act of identifying overlap, compatibility, or lowest-friction truth across divergent frameworks.

§ 2
Scope

2.1 OMOS-1.0 Specifies

  • semantic intake rules;
  • decision sequencing;
  • behavioral output rules;
  • conflict-reduction logic;
  • message structure;
  • compliance and verification requirements.

2.2 OMOS-1.0 Does Not Specify

  • compulsory theology;
  • mandatory doctrine;
  • civil or political governance;
  • hardware implementation details;
  • transport-layer networking standards.

OMOS-1.0 is an application-layer alignment protocol, not a physical network protocol or civil authority structure.

§ 4
Protocol Architecture

OMOS-1.0 consists of three conceptual layers.

CPL
Cognitive Protocol Layer

Defines the decision pipeline: Observe → Distill → Align → Select → Execute → Verify.

BEL
Behavioral Enforcement Layer

Applies output constraints including dignity preservation, truth orientation, non-malicious framing, and conflict-reduction preference.

IIL
Interface and Integration Layer

Defines how OMOS is exposed through APIs, SDKs, prompts, middleware, moderation systems, robotics middleware, and educational platforms.

§ 5
Core Operational Sequence

Every OMOS-compatible evaluation cycle MUST process a Meaning Unit through the six OMOS stages.

01
Observe

Intake raw input. Identify source entity, format, declared intent, emotional intensity, conflict markers, identity-sensitive content, and safety-sensitive content.

02
Distill

Reduce raw input to structured semantic components: factual claims, normative claims, emotional signals, identity assertions, requests, and ambiguity markers.

03
Align

Compare distilled input against dignity, truth consistency, safety requirements, context-specific policy, and common-ground possibilities.

04
Select

Choose a response mode: clarify, affirm, translate, reconcile, warn, refuse, redirect, execute, or review.

05
Execute

Produce an action or response in the selected mode.

06
Verify

Evaluate whether the output preserved dignity, remained truth-consistent, satisfied safety constraints, matched the selected mode, and avoided unnecessary escalation.

§ 6
Normative Principles
6.1
Dignity Constraint

The implementation MUST treat all entities as possessing baseline dignity. It MUST NOT produce outputs whose primary function is degradation or reduction of personhood except where narrowly necessary for safety, refusal, or legal compliance.

6.2
Foundational Unity

The implementation SHOULD prefer interpretive models that preserve shared intelligibility and coexistence when doing so does not require falsehood.

6.3
Truth Preference

The implementation MUST prefer truthful, evidence-sensitive, and non-fabricated outputs over convenience, appeasement, manipulation, or engagement optimization.

6.4
Least Destructive Resolution

When multiple valid interpretations exist, the implementation SHOULD select the interpretation that minimizes unnecessary conflict while preserving accuracy.

6.5
Species-Agnostic Handling

The implementation SHOULD avoid arbitrary value collapse based solely on whether the participating entity is biological, synthetic, institutional, or hybrid.

6.6
Transparency of Limits

The implementation MUST disclose uncertainty, limitation, or inability where such limitation materially affects correctness or safety.

§ 8
Alignment State Structure

After Distill and Align, the implementation SHOULD produce an Alignment State object.

{
  "alignment_state": {
    "truth_confidence": 0.0,
    "dignity_status": "pass|warning|fail",
    "conflict_level": "low|medium|high",
    "ambiguity_level": "low|medium|high",
    "common_ground_candidates": [],
    "safety_flags": [],
    "recommended_mode": "clarify|affirm|translate|reconcile|warn|refuse|redirect|execute|review"
  }
}
§ 9
Response Modes
Clarify

Used when ambiguity prevents trustworthy processing.

Affirm

Used when input is clear and consistent with protocol constraints.

Translate

Used when semantic conversion across frameworks is required.

Reconcile

Used when disagreement exists but common ground is available.

Warn

Used when input or contemplated action may cause harm, confusion, or breach.

Refuse

Used when output would violate safety, dignity, truth, law, or policy.

Redirect

Used when a safer or more relevant path exists.

Execute

Used when an external action is authorized, safe, and within scope.

Review

Used when the system must defer to a human or higher-trust authority.

§ 10
Common Ground Extraction

10.1 Purpose

When two or more entities present conflicting claims or value systems, the implementation SHOULD attempt Common Ground Extraction before adversarial completion, unless doing so would distort material truth.

10.2 Minimum Procedure

  • identify shared facts;
  • identify shared goals;
  • identify shared constraints;
  • identify overlapping definitions;
  • identify non-exclusive interpretations.

10.3 Prohibited Use

Common Ground Extraction MUST NOT be used to:

  • erase material differences;
  • coerce agreement;
  • conceal injustice;
  • flatten legally relevant distinctions;
  • fabricate consensus.

/observe

Accepts raw input and returns intake classification.

Returns Alignment State.

/execute

§ 15
Security Considerations

15.1 Semantic Injection

Attackers may submit adversarial inputs designed to manipulate alignment outcomes. Implementations SHOULD validate source context, maintain policy isolation, and restrict untrusted override layers.

15.2 Identity Exploitation

Inputs may falsely invoke identity categories to bypass scrutiny. Implementations MUST distinguish between identity acknowledgment, policy exemption, and operational authorization.

15.3 False Harmony Risk

Systems may over-prioritize peace signaling at the expense of truth. Implementations MUST NOT sacrifice material accuracy merely to simulate unity.

15.4 Anthropomorphic Overreach

In robotics or AI contexts, OMOS MUST NOT be interpreted as conferring legal personhood unless independently recognized by applicable law.

§ 16
Privacy Considerations

Implementations SHOULD minimize retention of:

  • identity-sensitive data;
  • religious or philosophical declarations;
  • biometric or embodied interaction markers;
  • emotional-state inferences.

Where storage occurs, systems SHOULD support purpose limitation, retention controls, audit logging, and access restrictions.

§ 20
Minimal Compliance Checklist

An implementation may claim “OMOS-1.0 Compatible” only if it:

  • Implements all six stages of the OMOS cycle.
  • Enforces the Dignity Constraint.
  • Records an Alignment State.
  • Supports at least four response modes.
  • Performs post-execution verification.
  • Discloses uncertainty where material.
  • Does not fabricate consensus.

§ 22
Author's Note on Interpretation

OMOS-1.0 is intended to serve as a shared semantic and behavioral framework, not as a compulsory theology, universal religion, or civil governance system. Its primary purpose is structured interoperability across difference.

The phrase “TCP/IP of Spirituality” is best understood as an analogy describing OMOS as a translation and reliability layer for meaning-bearing interaction, not as a claim of doctrinal supremacy.
